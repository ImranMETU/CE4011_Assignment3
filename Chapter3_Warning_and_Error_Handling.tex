\chapter{Warning and Error Handling}
\section{Robustness Strategy}

This chapter presents the Q3 robustness and diagnostic behavior of the developed solver. The objective is to ensure that invalid, unstable, or poorly defined structural models are detected before misleading numerical results are reported.

The global static analysis is based on the system
\[
\mathbf{K}\mathbf{d}=\mathbf{F},
\]
where the global stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ must be properly assembled, sufficiently constrained, and numerically stable in order to obtain a unique and physically meaningful solution.

For consistency in the Q3 demonstration cases, a common set of material and loading properties is adopted. Unless otherwise stated, frame members are modeled using reinforced concrete with elastic modulus
\[
E = 25 \times 10^{9}\ \text{Pa},
\]
and section properties
\[
A = 0.09\ \text{m}^2, \qquad I = 6.75 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^4.
\]
For truss members, the cross-sectional area is taken as
\[
A = 0.01\ \text{m}^2.
\]
A characteristic member length of
\[
L = 1.0\ \text{m}
\]
is used in the example structures. Applied loading is kept simple and consists of either a concentrated vertical point load of
\[
P = 10\ \text{kN}
\]
or a uniformly distributed load of
\[
w = 5\ \text{kN/m},
\]
depending on the structural configuration.

To improve reliability, the solver applies a layered validation procedure before and during analysis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Input checks:} detection of missing node IDs, invalid element references, and duplicate identifiers.
    \item \textbf{Property checks:} detection of nonphysical or nonpositive material and section properties, such as $E \leq 0$, $A \leq 0$, or invalid $I$ values for frame elements.
    \item \textbf{Geometry checks:} detection of zero-length elements and inconsistent element definitions.
    \item \textbf{Stability and constraint checks:} detection of unconstrained rigid-body motion, disconnected structural components, and zero-stiffness active degrees of freedom.
    \item \textbf{Solver checks:} detection of non-convergence or singular and near-singular stiffness behavior.
\end{itemize}

The solver’s response was verified against analytical solutions and external software (FTool), confirming correct identification of instability and mechanism behavior.
If any hard-failure condition is detected, analysis stops with an error message. If the model is solvable but suspicious (for example extreme conditioning), the run completes with warnings so that results are interpreted with caution.
Cases A and B are the point-load and uniformly distributed load verification cases, respectively, and the screenshots were produced in FTool, an open-source alternative to SAP.

\section{Structure Sample (a)}
\textbf{Model description:} Portal frame with insufficient lateral restraint, leading to a global sway mechanism.

\textbf{Physical interpretation:} The frame has fixed supports at the base (restrained in $u_x$, $u_y$, and $r_z$), but the top two nodes are unrestrained. Without sufficient lateral bracing or moment connection between the legs, the structure can sway horizontally. The degree of freedom count is: 2 free nodes × 3 DOF = 6, minus 3 restrained at base = 3 active degrees of freedom. With only axial/bending connections (no lateral bracing), the frame is deficient by 1 DOF, creating one rigid-body sway mode.

\textbf{Physical interpretation:} Two disconnected frame segments exist independently. The first segment has one stable configuration. \section{Structure Sample (c)}
\textbf{Model description:} Structure consisting of two disconnected but independently stable substructures.

\textbf{Physical interpretation:} Two substructures are disconnected (no direct load path between them), yet each is individually stable and statically determinate or indeterminate. The first substructure has $2 \times 3 - 3 = 3$ active DOFs. The second has similar stability. Since both are stable independently, the global problem can be solved, although the disconnection creates separate load paths and indicates unusual design. Nodal displacements, reactions, and member-end forces are still computable because the stiffness matrix is positive definite.

\section{Structure Sample (d)}
\textbf{Model description:} Two-member truss structure forming a stable triangular configuration.

\textbf{Physical interpretation:} The structure consists of two truss members connected at a top node, with fixed supports at the base nodes. The triangular geometry ($\Delta L = 0.5$ m horizontally, $\Delta h = 1$ m vertically) creates a statically determinate system with 1 free node ($3$ DOF) minus 0 restrained = 3 active DOF, reduced to 2 due to axial-only force carry ($u_x$, $u_y$). With 2 truss members and 2 active DOF, the system is determinate: $m = n_{\text{members}}, r = 2n_{\text{reactions}}$, so $m + r = 4$ constraints for 4 DOF (2 nodes × 2 translational DOF each). Vertical member loads are reacted by the axial-dominated truss geometry.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.50\textwidth]{q2_frame_analysis/images/ch3/model_d (1).png}
\caption{Structure Sample (d) model geometry}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Result or warning:} The determinacy check classifies the system as statically determinate. The analysis proceeds successfully, producing nodal displacements, support reactions, and axial member forces. Truss elements correctly carry only axial forces (shear force $V_y = 0$, moments $M_z = 0$), confirming correct pin-jointed truss behavior.

\textbf{Equilibrium verification:} The computed reactions are:
\begin{align*}
\sum F_x &= R_{x,1} + R_{x,2} = 0 \text{ (horizontal equilibrium)}, \\
\sum F_y &= R_{y,1} + R_{y,2} - \text{(total applied load)} = 0 \text{ (vertical equilibrium)}, \\
\sum M &= R_{x,1} \cdot \Delta h + \cdots = 0 \text{ (moment equilibrium about any point)}.
\end{align*}
All equilibrium checks pass within numerical precision, confirming the solver's correctness.

\section{Structure Sample (e)}
\textbf{Model description:} Beam structure with an internal hinge introducing a moment release within the system.

\textbf{Physical interpretation:} A continuous beam with three nodes is cut by an internal hinge at node 2, releasing the bending moment at that location. The structure has $3 \times 3 - (3 + 0) = 6$ active DOF globally. With the hinge (moment release), one constraint is added, reducing the rank of the stiffness matrix. The system becomes statically indeterminate of degree 1. However, the hinge creates a known zero-moment condition at the release point, which the solver enforces through the condensed stiffness formulation. Physically, the beam behaves like two simply-supported spans meeting at a pin: each span is locally determinate, and the combined structure is indeterminate by 1 (a reaction can be chosen freely, others follow).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection*{Validation and Solution Workflow}

The robustness validation workflow employed by the solver follows a systematic decision path from input parsing through solution and error detection. Key validation stages include input syntax checking, geometry validation (zero-length elements), property checks ($E > 0$, $A > 0$, $I \geq 0$), degree-of-freedom assignment and numbering, determinacy analysis, global stiffness assembly, singular matrix detection, and numerical solution. At each stage, errors trigger immediate termination with informative messages, while warnings allow analysis to proceed when structures are flagged as suspicious but solvable. This layered approach ensures that only physically and numerically sound structures produce results, while potentially problematic cases are caught early.
